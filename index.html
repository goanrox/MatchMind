Soccer 101

The rules, explained
like American sports

Six things every new fan needs to understand before kick-off. Read them once and you'll get the game.

⏱️

How Time Works

Two 45-minute halves with a running clock that never stops. The ref adds "stoppage time" at the end of each half for delays. Matches almost always run 92–97 minutes total.

🏀 Like in basketball

Imagine the NBA clock running through fouls and timeouts — then the ref added 4 minutes at the end to make up for all of it.

✓ Beginner
🟨

Yellow & Red Cards

Yellow card = formal warning. Two yellows in one game = automatic red. A red means immediate ejection AND your team plays shorthanded (10 vs 11) for the rest of the match. No replacement allowed.

🏀 Like in basketball

Two technical fouls and you're gone — except your whole team suffers by playing one man down for the rest of the game. No equivalent in American sports.

⚠ Know This
📊

How Standings Work

Win = 3 points. Draw = 1 point each. Loss = 0. The top 2 teams from each group of 4 advance to the knockout round. It's only 3 games — every single match matters enormously.

⚾ Like in baseball

Like a 3-game series where the division winner AND wild card both advance — except there's absolutely no second chance if you lose two.

✓ Beginner
🏆

Extra Time & Shootouts

Tied after 90 minutes in a knockout game? Two more 15-minute periods (extra time). Still level? Five players each take penalty kicks alternating. If tied after five — sudden death kicks continue until someone misses.

🏒 Like in hockey

OT period then a shootout — except everyone's sobbing regardless of the outcome and a shootout deciding a World Cup is genuinely traumatic.

★ Key Moment
📐

Formations Explained

Teams line up in formations like 4-3-3 (4 defenders, 3 midfielders, 3 forwards). The goalkeeper is always separate. Coaches switch formations mid-game based on the score and opponent.

🏈 Like in football

Offensive and defensive play schemes — a 4-3-3 is aggressive, a 5-4-1 "parks the bus." Coaches adjust exactly like an NFL play-caller at halftime.

⚠ Intermediate
🎯

Set Pieces & Dead Balls

Set pieces are pre-planned plays from dead ball situations: free kicks, corner kicks, and throw-ins. Teams practice elaborate routines for these — they account for roughly 30% of all top-level goals.

🏈 Like in football

Scripted plays from a dead ball — a corner kick is an inbound play, a free kick is either a field goal attempt or a trick play depending on the distance.

🎓 Soccer School

12 lessons that explain soccer using American sports. Tap any lesson to read it.

1. ⚠️ The Offside Rule

You're offside if you're past the second-to-last defender (usually the last outfield player) when the ball is played to you. The rule prevents "goal hanging." Timing is everything — if you're level, you're onside.

American Analogy: Like being in the crease before the puck in hockey. You can't camp ahead of the play. The linesman's flag goes up, the goal is disallowed, and play restarts with the defending team.

Exception: You can't be offside from a corner kick, throw-in, or goal kick. And being in your own half is always fine.

2. 🟨 Yellow & Red Cards

Yellow = caution/warning. Second yellow in one game = automatic red. Red = immediate ejection. Your team plays 10 vs 11 for the rest of the match — no substitution allowed to replace the sent-off player.

Direct red cards are given for violent conduct, serious foul play, denying an obvious goal-scoring opportunity (DOGSO), or spitting.

American Analogy: Two technical fouls in the NBA ejects you — but in soccer, your whole team suffers the 10-man disadvantage for the rest of the game. No parallel in American sports.

3. 🎯 Penalty Kicks

A penalty is awarded when a player is fouled inside the opponent's 18-yard penalty box. The kick is taken from the penalty spot — 12 yards from goal — one-on-one with the goalkeeper. All other players stand outside the box.

The keeper must stay on the line until the ball is kicked. Kickers score approximately 75–78% of penalties at the highest level.

American Analogy: Like a free throw — but it's a full breakaway and far more dramatic. The entire crowd holds its breath. World Cups are decided by these.

4. 📺 VAR Explained

VAR (Video Assistant Referee) is a team of officials watching every play on multiple camera feeds. They can alert the on-field referee to potential errors in four categories: goals, penalty decisions, direct red cards, and mistaken identity.

The on-field referee can then go to a pitchside monitor to review the footage and make a final call. The process typically takes 1–3 minutes.

American Analogy: Exactly like the NFL replay official in the booth, or an NHL situation room call from Toronto. Same concept — officiating support via video review.

5. ⏱️ Stoppage Time

The referee tracks time lost during the half to injuries, substitutions, goal celebrations, VAR reviews, and time-wasting. At the end of the half, the fourth official holds up a board showing the minimum added time (e.g., "+5 minutes").

The ref can play longer than announced if events (another injury, a goal, VAR review) occur during stoppage time itself. This is why matches sometimes go to 97, 99, or even 103 minutes.

American Analogy: Like NBA makeup time — except the amount isn't precise and the ref decides it on the fly, based on everything that happened during the half.

6. 🏆 Extra Time & Shootouts

In knockout rounds, if the score is level after 90 minutes, two extra 15-minute periods are played (30 minutes total). There is no "golden goal" — both periods must be completed.

If still level after 120 minutes, a penalty shootout decides the match. Five players per team alternate kicks. If tied after five each, sudden death continues until one team scores and the other misses.

American Analogy: Hockey overtime then a shootout — except the stakes are infinitely higher and grown adults openly cry when their team loses on penalties.

7. 📐 Formations & Tactics

A formation describes where the 10 outfield players are positioned. Numbers go back-to-front: 4-3-3 means 4 defenders, 3 midfielders, 3 forwards. The goalkeeper is always separate and not counted.

Common formations:
• 4-3-3: Attacking, wide, used by Barcelona and Liverpool
• 4-4-2: Balanced classic, easy to understand
• 3-5-2: Wing-heavy, unpredictable
• 5-4-1: Ultra-defensive "park the bus"

American Analogy: NFL offensive/defensive schemes — coaches switch mid-game just like a play-caller adjusts based on the score and opponent.

8. 🔄 Substitutions

Teams can make up to 5 substitutions per game from a bench of up to 15 players (varies by competition). Subs can only be made during a stoppage in play. Once a player comes off, they cannot return.

In extra time, an additional (6th) substitute is allowed. Substitutions are often tactical — bringing on a faster attacker when chasing the game, or a defensive midfielder to protect a lead.

American Analogy: Like a basketball substitution — except you only get 5 all game and taking a player off is permanent. Managers agonize over every one.

9. 🏁 Corner Kicks

A corner kick is awarded when the ball crosses the goal line (not between the posts) and was last touched by a defending player. The attacking team places the ball in the quarter-circle arc in the corner of the field and kicks it in.

Teams often practice elaborate corner routines — near-post runs, blockers, back-post headers. Approximately 2–3 goals per game at major tournaments come from corners.

American Analogy: Like an inbound play in basketball after the ball goes out of bounds off the defense — you get possession and run a set play.

10. 📋 Group Stage & Knockout

In 2026, 48 teams are split into 12 groups of 4. Each team plays the other 3 teams once. Points: Win=3, Draw=1, Loss=0. The top 2 from each group (plus 8 best third-place teams) advance to the Round of 32.

From there it's single-elimination. One loss and you go home. The bracket proceeds: Round of 32 → Round of 16 → Quarterfinals → Semifinals → Final.

American Analogy: MLB divisional race (group stage) then March Madness single-elimination (knockout). Except with 3.5 billion viewers watching the Final.

11. 🧤 The Goalkeeper's Rules

The goalkeeper is the only player allowed to use their hands — but only inside their own penalty box. Outside the box, they're treated like any outfield player.

Goalkeepers cannot pick up a ball deliberately passed back to them by a teammate's foot (back-pass rule). They have 6 seconds to release the ball once holding it. They wear a different colored jersey to distinguish them from both teams.

American Analogy: Like the one designated hitter position — specific rules that only apply to one player on the field, and violating them costs your team.

12. 🌍 World Cup Format 2026

For the first time ever, the 2026 World Cup features 48 teams — expanded from 32. It's hosted across 3 countries (USA, Canada, Mexico) and 16 cities. The USA has 11 host cities alone.

Key cities: New York/NJ, Los Angeles, Dallas, San Francisco, Seattle, Boston, Miami, Atlanta, Houston, Kansas City, Philadelphia (USA) + Toronto, Vancouver (Canada) + Mexico City, Guadalajara, Monterrey (Mexico).

The Final will be held at MetLife Stadium in East Rutherford, NJ on July 19, 2026 — the largest World Cup Final venue ever.

🕵️ Match Integrity

🎰 What is Match-Fixing?

Match-fixing occurs when players, referees, or officials are bribed to influence the outcome of a game for gambling profits. Underground betting syndicates — primarily based in Southeast Asia — run operations worth billions annually. Referees may be targeted to make key calls at specific moments (a yellow card at the 30th minute, a penalty in the second half) that affect betting markets without obviously determining the final score.

FIFA, Interpol, and Sportradar operate 24/7 monitoring of betting lines globally. Unusual betting spikes before games are flagged and investigated.

🇮🇹 Calciopoli 2006 — Italy's Biggest Scandal

In 2006, wiretapped phone recordings revealed that Juventus director Luciano Moggi had been systematically influencing referee assignments in Serie A — Italy's top division. Moggi selected favorable referees for key matches and arranged for hostile officials to be assigned to rivals.

The fallout: Juventus were stripped of two Serie A titles and relegated to Serie B. AC Milan, Fiorentina, Lazio, and Reggina were all docked points. It remains the most significant corruption scandal in European club football history, verified by the Italian Football Federation (FIGC).

👀 Red Flags — How to Spot a Suspicious Game

Watch for: unusual betting line movements in the hours before kick-off; obvious unforced errors by normally reliable players; goalkeeper positioning that defies basic logic on easy shots; a team not pressing despite high stakes; a scoreline that mirrors suspicious pre-match odds; or any reported news of a player's financial difficulties or gambling debts.

None of these alone prove fixing — but combinations of multiple flags during the same game are what Sportradar's Early Warning System is designed to catch.

🛡️ How FIFA & Sportradar Fight Back

Sportradar monitors over 60,000 matches per year across 500+ competitions using algorithmic detection of abnormal betting patterns. FIFA's Integrity Unit operates a confidential whistleblower hotline. All players and officials at World Cup 2026 undergo anti-corruption briefings before the tournament.

Penalties for proven match-fixing include lifetime bans from all football activity. Several lower-league European players have received lifetime bans in recent years. The deterrent is real — but so is the money involved at the highest level.

📜 Famous World Cup Controversies

1966: England's 3rd goal vs Germany — linesman Tofiq Bahramov awarded a goal that may not have crossed the line. Still debated 60 years later.

1986: Maradona's "Hand of God" — scored with his fist, never admitted it until years later. Argentina beat England and went on to win the tournament.

2002: South Korea's run to the semifinals — referee decisions against Italy and Spain are still widely questioned by historians.

2010: Frank Lampard's ghost goal vs Germany — ball clearly crossed the line, not given. Directly led to the introduction of goal-line technology.

2022: Qatar hosting rights — a US Department of Justice investigation found evidence of bribery in the 2010 FIFA vote that awarded Qatar the World Cup.
