"Why is everyone celebrating a 0-0 tie??"

The Answer
A 0-0 game is often a tactical war — two defenses refusing to crack. Keeping a clean sheet away from home is a massive result; it means the return leg is on your terms.
🏈 American Analogy

Like a 3-0 NFL game where the defense dominated every drive — fans of the winning defense go wild even though no one scored.

"What even is offside? I've asked 10 times."
↑ Hover for the answer
The Answer
You're offside if you're past the last defender at the exact moment your teammate passes to you — giving you an unfair head start toward goal.
🏒 American Analogy

Exactly like being in the crease before the puck in hockey — you can't be there before the play arrives. Same concept, different sport.

🤷
"Why did the clock go past 90 minutes??"
↑ Hover for the answer
The Answer
The clock never officially stops — it just keeps running. The ref announces "stoppage time" at the end to make up for minutes lost to injuries, subs, and VAR.
🏀 American Analogy

Like the NBA adding back timeout time at the end of a quarter — except the ref eyeballs it and holds up a board showing "+4 minutes."

"Why did the game just stop for a VIDEO REPLAY??"

The Answer
That's VAR — Video Assistant Referee. Officials watch every play on multiple angles and flag the ref if there's a clear error on goals, penalties, or red cards.
🏈 American Analogy

Exactly like NFL instant replay — the ref walks to a pitchside monitor, watches the footage, then makes the final call. Fans hate the 2-minute wait. It's necessary.

😳
"Why did they get a 1-on-1 shot after a foul??"

The Answer
That's a penalty kick — awarded when a player is fouled inside the 18-yard box. One player vs the goalkeeper from 12 yards. Kickers score about 75% of the time.
🏀 American Analogy

Like a technical foul free throw — except it's a full breakaway with enormous pressure. Entire World Cups have been won and lost by them.

Like an NBA player flopping to draw a charge — some are real, some theatrical. Soccer players just tend to stay down longer and look more dramatic about it.

Soccer 101

The rules, explained
like American sports

Six things every new fan needs to understand before kick-off. Read them once and you'll get the game.

⏱️

How Time Works

Two 45-minute halves with a running clock that never stops. The ref adds "stoppage time" at the end of each half for delays. Matches almost always run 92–97 minutes total.

🏀 Like in basketball

Imagine the NBA clock running through fouls and timeouts — then the ref added 4 minutes at the end to make up for all of it.

✓ Beginner
🟨

Yellow & Red Cards

Yellow card = formal warning. Two yellows in one game = automatic red. A red means immediate ejection AND your team plays shorthanded (10 vs 11) for the rest of the match. No replacement allowed.

🏀 Like in basketball

Two technical fouls and you're gone — except your whole team suffers by playing one man down for the rest of the game. No equivalent in American sports.

⚠ Know This
📊

How Standings Work

Win = 3 points. Draw = 1 point each. Loss = 0. The top 2 teams from each group of 4 advance to the knockout round. It's only 3 games — every single match matters enormously.

⚾ Like in baseball

Like a 3-game series where the division winner AND wild card both advance — except there's absolutely no second chance if you lose two.

✓ Beginner
🏆

Extra Time & Shootouts

Tied after 90 minutes in a knockout game? Two more 15-minute periods (extra time). Still level? Five players each take penalty kicks alternating. If tied after five — sudden death kicks continue until someone misses.

🏒 Like in hockey

OT period then a shootout — except everyone's sobbing regardless of the outcome and a shootout deciding a World Cup is genuinely traumatic.

★ Key Moment
📐

Formations Explained

Teams line up in formations like 4-3-3 (4 defenders, 3 midfielders, 3 forwards). The goalkeeper is always separate. Coaches switch formations mid-game based on the score and opponent.

🏈 Like in football

Offensive and defensive play schemes — a 4-3-3 is aggressive, a 5-4-1 "parks the bus." Coaches adjust exactly like an NFL play-caller at halftime.

⚠ Intermediate
🎯

Set Pieces & Dead Balls

Set pieces are pre-planned plays from dead ball situations: free kicks, corner kicks, and throw-ins. Teams practice elaborate routines for these — they account for roughly 30% of all top-level goals.

🏈 Like in football

Scripted plays from a dead ball — a corner kick is an inbound play, a free kick is either a field goal attempt or a trick play depending on the distance.

★ Key Moment

🎓 Soccer School

12 lessons that explain soccer using American sports. Tap any lesson to read it.

1. ⚠️ The Offside Rule

You're offside if you're past the second-to-last defender (usually the last outfield player) when the ball is played to you. The rule prevents "goal hanging." Timing is everything — if you're level, you're onside.

American Analogy: Like being in the crease before the puck in hockey. You can't camp ahead of the play. The linesman's flag goes up, the goal is disallowed, and play restarts with the defending team.

Exception: You can't be offside from a corner kick, throw-in, or goal kick. And being in your own half is always fine.

2. 🟨 Yellow & Red Cards

Yellow = caution/warning. Second yellow in one game = automatic red. Red = immediate ejection. Your team plays 10 vs 11 for the rest of the match — no substitution allowed to replace the sent-off player.

Direct red cards are given for violent conduct, serious foul play, denying an obvious goal-scoring opportunity (DOGSO), or spitting.

American Analogy: Two technical fouls in the NBA ejects you — but in soccer, your whole team suffers the 10-man disadvantage for the rest of the game. No parallel in American sports.

3. 🎯 Penalty Kicks

A penalty is awarded when a player is fouled inside the opponent's 18-yard penalty box. The kick is taken from the penalty spot — 12 yards from goal — one-on-one with the goalkeeper. All other players stand outside the box.

The keeper must stay on the line until the ball is kicked. Kickers score approximately 75–78% of penalties at the highest level.

American Analogy: Like a free throw — but it's a full breakaway and far more dramatic. The entire crowd holds its breath. World Cups are decided by these.

4. 📺 VAR Explained

VAR (Video Assistant Referee) is a team of officials watching every play on multiple camera feeds. They can alert the on-field referee to potential errors in four categories: goals, penalty decisions, direct red cards, and mistaken identity.

The on-field referee can then go to a pitchside monitor to review the footage and make a final call. The process typically takes 1–3 minutes.

American Analogy: Exactly like the NFL replay official in the booth, or an NHL situation room call from Toronto. Same concept — officiating support via video review.

5. ⏱️ Stoppage Time

The referee tracks time lost during the half to injuries, substitutions, goal celebrations, VAR reviews, and time-wasting. At the end of the half, the fourth official holds up a board showing the minimum added time (e.g., "+5 minutes").

The ref can play longer than announced if events occur during stoppage time itself. This is why matches sometimes go to 97, 99, or even 103 minutes.

American Analogy: Like NBA makeup time — except the amount isn't precise and the ref decides it on the fly.

6. 🏆 Extra Time & Shootouts

In knockout rounds, if the score is level after 90 minutes, two extra 15-minute periods are played. If still level after 120 minutes, a penalty shootout decides the match.

American Analogy: Hockey overtime then a shootout — except the stakes are infinitely higher and grown adults openly cry when their team loses on penalties.

7. 📐 Formations & Tactics

A formation describes where the 10 outfield players are positioned. Numbers go back-to-front: 4-3-3 means 4 defenders, 3 midfielders, 3 forwards.

American Analogy: NFL offensive/defensive schemes — coaches switch mid-game just like a play-caller adjusts based on the score and opponent.

8. 🔄 Substitutions

Teams can make up to 5 substitutions per game. Once a player comes off, they cannot return.

American Analogy: Like a basketball substitution — except you only get 5 all game and taking a player off is permanent.

9. 🏁 Corner Kicks

A corner kick is awarded when the ball crosses the goal line and was last touched by a defending player.

American Analogy: Like an inbound play in basketball after the ball goes out of bounds off the defense.

10. 📋 Group Stage & Knockout

In 2026, 48 teams are split into groups. Points: Win=3, Draw=1, Loss=0. The top teams advance, then it becomes single-elimination.

American Analogy: MLB divisional race then March Madness single-elimination.

11. 🧤 The Goalkeeper's Rules

The goalkeeper is the only player allowed to use their hands — but only inside their own penalty box. Outside the box, they're treated like any outfield player.

12. 🌍 World Cup Format 2026

For the first time ever, the 2026 World Cup features 48 teams — expanded from 32. It's hosted across 3 countries (USA, Canada, Mexico) and 16 cities.

🕵️ Match Integrity

🎰 What is Match-Fixing?

Match-fixing occurs when players, referees, or officials are bribed to influence the outcome of a game for gambling profits. Underground betting syndicates run operations worth billions annually.

🇮🇹 Calciopoli 2006 — Italy's Biggest Scandal

In 2006, wiretapped phone recordings revealed systematic influence over referee assignments in Serie A. Juventus were stripped of titles and relegated.

👀 Red Flags — How to Spot a Suspicious Game

Watch for unusual betting line movement, strange unforced errors, and tactics that make no competitive sense.

🛡️ How FIFA & Sportradar Fight Back

Monitoring systems, data analysis, and integrity hotlines help flag suspicious matches and protect the sport.
